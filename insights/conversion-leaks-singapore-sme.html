The 7 Conversion Leaks I Find on Every Singapore SME Website (And How to Plug Them Without a Redesign)

After reviewing dozens of Singapore business websites, the same seven problems appear almost every time. None of them require an expensive redesign. Most take under an hour to fix once you know where to look.

TLDR — Key Points
  • The same seven conversion leaks appear on almost every Singapore SME website — none require a redesign to fix
  • The root cause is always the same: the website was built from the business owner's perspective, not the customer's
  • Leak 1: No clear call to action above the fold — visitors don't know what to do next
  • Leak 2: The headline talks about the business, not the customer's problem or outcome
  • Leak 3: The contact form has too many fields — every extra field loses customers at the last step
  • Leak 4: Social proof is missing or buried — trust signals belong near the top, not the footer
  • Leak 5: The website is slow on mobile — over 60% of Singapore web traffic is on a phone
  • Leak 6: The phone number is hard to find — make it visible on every page, tap-to-call on mobile
  • Leak 7: Paid traffic is going to the homepage — dedicated landing pages convert 2–5x better for ads

A website that doesn't convert is not a design problem. It's a decision problem.

Every time a visitor lands on your website and leaves without calling, booking, or enquiring, a decision was made — just not the one you wanted. Something on the page made them hesitate. Something was missing that they needed. Something confused them just enough to make leaving feel easier than staying.

The good news is that these friction points follow patterns. After reviewing Singapore SME websites across dental, renovation, legal, F&B, trades, and professional services, the same seven problems appear with remarkable consistency. None of them require rebuilding your website from scratch. Most can be fixed today.

1
Nobody knows what to do when they land on your page
Open your website right now and look at it as if you've never seen it before. Ask yourself one question: within five seconds, is it completely obvious what I should do next? On most Singapore SME websites, the answer is no. The homepage has a banner image, a tagline, a navigation menu with six items, and maybe a "Learn More" button that goes to an About page. There's no single, clear, prominent action for the visitor to take. Visitors don't browse websites the way we imagine they do. They scan for relevance and a clear next step. If neither is immediately obvious, they leave.
The fix: Every page on your website needs one primary call to action that stands out visually from everything else. For most Singapore service businesses, that's a phone number, a booking button, or an enquiry form. Pick one. Make it impossible to miss. Put it above the fold — before the visitor scrolls a single pixel.
2
Your headline is about you, not them
Read the first headline on your homepage. Does it start with "We," "Our," or your business name? If it does, you're leading with the wrong subject. The person landing on your website arrived because they have a problem or a need. They are not yet interested in your story, your team, or your commitment to excellence. They are interested in one thing: whether you can help them. A headline that says "Singapore's trusted renovation specialists since 2005" is about you. A headline that says "Your renovation completed on time, on budget, and without the usual stress" is about them. The second version converts at a meaningfully higher rate — not because it's cleverer, but because it speaks to what the visitor actually cares about in the first five seconds.
The fix: Rewrite your homepage headline so it describes the outcome the customer gets, or the problem you solve. Test it by asking: does this make a first-time visitor feel like you understand their situation?
3
Your contact form asks for too much
Count the fields in your enquiry form. If the answer is more than four, you are losing customers at the last step — the moment when they've decided they want to contact you but haven't yet committed. Every additional field in a form is a small moment of friction. Each one gives the visitor a reason to pause, reconsider, or simply close the tab. A form with eight fields asking for name, email, phone, preferred date, preferred time, service required, budget range, and how they heard about you will convert at a fraction of the rate of a form asking for name, phone, and what they need help with. You do not need all that information upfront. You need enough to call them back.
The fix: Reduce your contact form to three or four fields maximum. Name, phone number or email, and one open field for their message or requirement. If you must include more, make the additional fields optional and say so clearly.
4
You have no social proof where it matters
Most Singapore business websites either have no testimonials at all, or have them buried at the bottom of the page after the visitor has already decided to leave. Social proof is not a nice addition to your website. It is a conversion mechanism. It answers the question every first-time visitor is silently asking before they decide to contact you: "Has this worked for someone like me?" The placement matters as much as the content. A testimonial buried in the footer does almost nothing. The same testimonial placed directly below your headline, or immediately above your contact form, converts visitors who were on the fence into enquiries. Read more about how Google reviews drive conversion — they're the most trusted form of social proof for Singapore businesses.
The fix: Move your best testimonial to immediately below your hero section. Add your Google review count somewhere visible above the fold. The most effective trust signals: Google review count and rating, specific testimonials with real outcomes, recognisable client logos.
5
Your website is slow on mobile — and you haven't checked
More than 60% of web traffic in Singapore comes from mobile devices. For local service businesses, it's often higher — people search for a dentist or a plumber on their phone, right when they need one. If your website takes more than three seconds to load on a mobile connection, a significant portion of your visitors are leaving before they've seen a single word of your content. The most common causes of slow mobile loading on Singapore SME websites are oversized images that were never compressed, plugins or scripts that load in the background, and websites that were built for desktop and adapted for mobile as an afterthought.
The fix: Test your website right now on Google's free PageSpeed Insights tool at pagespeed.web.dev. Enter your URL and look at the mobile score. Anything below 70 is worth addressing. The single most impactful quick fix for most Singapore websites is image compression.
6
Visitors can't find your phone number fast enough
Visit ten Singapore SME websites right now and count how many have their phone number visible without scrolling or clicking. Most don't. The phone number is in the footer. Or it's on the Contact page. Or it requires the visitor to click "Get in touch" and fill in a form before any contact details appear. For local service businesses in Singapore, the phone call is often the preferred contact method — especially for older customers, for urgent needs, and for higher-value services where the customer wants to speak to someone before committing. Making them hunt for your number means losing customers who would have called if the number had been in front of them.
The fix: Put your phone number in the top right corner of every page, in text large enough to read without squinting. On mobile, make it a tap-to-call link so visitors can call you with one tap. This change alone has a measurable impact on enquiry rates.
7
You're sending paid traffic to your homepage
If you're running Google Ads and sending visitors to your homepage, you're wasting a significant portion of your ad budget. Your homepage is designed to introduce your business to someone who found you organically. It has navigation, multiple sections, and content serving different purposes for different visitors. It's not designed to convert someone who clicked a specific ad for a specific service. A visitor who clicked an ad for "Invisalign consultation Singapore" and lands on your homepage has to navigate, find the relevant service, and then decide to contact you. Every step between the ad click and the enquiry loses people. A dedicated landing page — one page, one service, one clear action — converts at two to five times the rate of a homepage for paid traffic.
The fix: If you're running Google Ads, build a dedicated landing page for each campaign. Clear headline, three to five key points about the service, social proof, and a prominent contact method. This is one of the highest-return CRO investments for Singapore businesses running paid traffic.

The common thread

Every one of these seven leaks has the same root cause: the website was built from the business owner's perspective, not the customer's. The business owner knows what they offer, trusts their own reputation, and understands how to navigate their own website. The first-time visitor knows none of this. They're making quick judgements with incomplete information, and every moment of confusion tips the balance towards leaving.

Fixing conversion leaks is not about making your website prettier. It's about removing the friction that stands between a visitor's interest and the action you want them to take. See the full CRO service options if you'd like help identifying and fixing the leaks that are costing you the most.

Key Takeaways
  • Every conversion leak has the same root cause — friction between a visitor's interest and the action you want them to take
  • One primary call to action above the fold, visually distinct from everything else on the page
  • Rewrite your homepage headline to describe the customer's outcome, not your credentials
  • Reduce your contact form to 3–4 fields maximum — name, phone or email, and what they need
  • Move your best testimonial to immediately below your hero section — not the footer
  • Test your mobile load speed at pagespeed.web.dev — anything below 70 needs attention
  • Your phone number belongs in the top right corner of every page, as a tap-to-call link on mobile
