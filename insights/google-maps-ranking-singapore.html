How Google Maps actually decides who ranks

Google Maps uses three things to decide who shows up at the top. Most business owners only know about one of them.

Relevance

Does your Google Maps listing match what the person searched for? Google looks at your business category, the services you've listed, your business description, and even the words you use in your weekly posts. A dental clinic that lists "teeth whitening," "Invisalign," "dental implants," and "emergency dentist" will show up in far more searches than one that simply says "dentist."

Distance

How close is your business to the person searching? You can't move your shop. But you can influence this through how you set your service area, the locations you mention in your content, and making sure your address appears consistently everywhere online.

Prominence

How well-known does Google think you are? This one trips up the most businesses. Prominence is built through your Google reviews (how many you have and how recent they are), how authoritative your website is, and how consistently your business details appear across the internet.

Key insight

Most Singapore SMEs have a half-filled Google Maps listing, no strategy for building reviews, and haven't touched their profile in months. That's why rank #7 is so common. And it's also why fixing it is more about execution than expertise.

Six reasons you're stuck at #7

1. Your Google Maps listing is half empty

Google gives you a lot of space to describe your business — your services, your hours, your photos, a full description, answers to common questions, weekly updates. Most Singapore businesses fill in their name, address, phone number, and maybe their category, then leave everything else blank.

Think of it this way: if Google is deciding between you and a competitor, and your competitor has filled in every field while yours is half empty, who do you think gets the nudge?

Fill in everything. Write a proper business description. List every service you offer. Upload at least 10 photos — your shopfront, your team, your work. Answer the questions customers actually ask.

2. You have fewer reviews — and yours are older

Research from BrightLocal shows that businesses with 50 or more Google reviews get 266% more leads than businesses with fewer than 10. But the number isn't the only thing that matters — how recently you got them does too.

A business with 80 reviews, the last one from eight months ago, will often rank below a business with 30 reviews and one from last week. Google reads fresh reviews as a sign that the business is active and still delivering.

The fix is simple but requires consistency: ask every happy customer to leave a review, within a day or two of their visit. Send them a direct link so it takes 30 seconds, not five minutes. Read more about building a Google review system that works.

3. Your business details are different everywhere online

Your business name, address, and phone number need to be identical everywhere they appear — your Google listing, your website, Yelp, Facebook, industry directories, everywhere. If Google sees "ABC Dental Pte Ltd" on one site, "ABC Dental" on another, and "ABC Dental Singapore" on a third, it treats them as three different businesses. This inconsistency suppresses your Maps rank because Google cannot confidently confirm your identity.

For Singapore businesses, the most critical directories to get consistent are: Google Business Profile, your own website footer, Yelp Singapore, Yellow Pages Singapore, and any industry-specific directories relevant to your sector.

4. Your website doesn't have enough pages

Your Google Maps ranking and your website are connected. Google uses your website to understand how credible and relevant your business is. A website with five pages sends a very different signal than one with thirty.

Look at the dental clinics ranking at the top in Singapore. They typically have a page for each treatment they offer, pages targeting specific neighbourhoods, and content that answers the questions patients search for. That's 20 to 40 pages versus the five or eight that most Singapore SMEs have.

5. You haven't posted on Google Maps in months

Google lets you publish updates directly on your Maps listing — offers, news, photos, events. Posting regularly tells Google your business is active. Not posting tells Google the opposite.

If your last update was six months ago and a competitor posts every week, Google will favour them. One post a week is enough. It doesn't need to be long — a current promotion, a new service, a recent photo with a short caption. Read the full Google Business Profile optimisation checklist for everything that matters.

6. You're listed under the wrong category

Your primary business category on Google Maps is one of the most heavily weighted ranking signals. Choosing something too broad — "Health" instead of "Dental Clinic" — means you're competing for searches you can't win, while missing the ones you should be showing up for.

Check what categories your top-ranked competitors are using. Make sure yours is at least as specific.

What a well-ranked listing actually looks like

The businesses sitting at number one in competitive Singapore categories didn't get there by accident. If you look at what they have in common, the pattern is clear:

  • Their Google Maps listing is completely filled in and updated every week
  • They're getting one to three new reviews consistently, week after week
  • Their business name, address, and phone number are identical across every directory
  • Their website has dedicated pages for every service and every area they serve
  • Their business details are confirmed by schema markup on their website

None of this is technically difficult. What makes it hard is that it needs to happen every week, across multiple things at once, without stopping. Most business owners simply don't have that time — which is why it doesn't get done.

The compound effect

Each signal reinforces the others. More reviews improve prominence. More posts improve relevance. More content pages improve website authority. The system compounds — which is why businesses that build it properly tend to stay at #1 once they get there.

How long does it take to move up?

Businesses that address all of these areas typically start seeing their ranking move within six to ten weeks. Getting into the top three takes three to four months in most categories.

In more competitive spaces — dental, legal, home renovation, financial services — expect six to nine months to reach and hold a top three position. The businesses that get there faster are the ones that do more of this, more consistently, from the start.

Before you invest in any local SEO service in Singapore, understand your current position clearly. What's your rank right now? How many reviews do you have versus your top competitors? How complete is your listing?
